###1 - Introduction

Historical claims on the existence of resistance inside of the camp. The author state that when we forget such stories, we "forget about our own capacities for resistance".
We live in (or rather haunt) a dead civilization, an all-devouring system controlling us through screens, considering us free because we can vote an consume (paraphrasing Conspiracy of Cells of Fire).
But in both cases, we (either "we, today" or the victims of the Holocaust) are resisting : "Then perhaps a better question might be: Why are we continuously being led to our slaughter like sheep ?, to which many of us simply reply: We aren't."

#### AnNihil

A summary of the AnNihil stance :

>The anarcho-nihilist position is essentially that we are fucked. That the current manifestation of human society (civilization, leviathan, industrial society, global capitalism, whatever) is beyond salvation, and so our response to it should be one of unmitigated hostility. There are no demands to be made, no utopic visions to be upheld, no political programs to be followed - the path of resistance is one of pure negation. In short, 'that conditions in the social organization are so bad as to make destruction desirable for its own sake inde­pendent of any constructive program or possibility.'

Same sentiment in 19th Russia nihilist milieu (no program, pure negation).

#### Collision (of AnNihil and concentration camp resistance)

"Recalcitrance " : Resisting a authority or control; not obedient or compliant; hard to manage.
This book is not about reinventing nihilism/anarchism or propose an alternative view on the Holocaust.

>At heart, this book is about tapping into the instinctual rebelliousness that resides underneath of every organization, affinity group, project, and action that we participate in; that reflexive spirit of resis­tance rooted in the basic existential understanding that recalcitrance is simply a more meaningful and joyous form of existence than docility.

Nihilism puts meaning over success, embracing feelings of cynicism, hopelessness in order to "cultivate immediate joy and moments of liberation."

>Whether we succeed in overthrowing our oppressors and bringing about a brighter future can only be sec­ondary to the visceral need to rebel against the shitty conditions of our lives."

#### The Ceaseless Lager

This is not a book about happy endings, almost all stories of resistance end with mass torture, slaughter, and enslave­ment.
Acknowledgement of the limits of comparing WWII camps and the 21st century.
Interesting excerpt :

>It's important to remember that the Nazis didn't have to build all of their own camps (some of that work was done by the Social Democratic governments prior) , nor did they have to decommission all of them after the war (the Soviets put a couple of them to good use).

Mentions of atrocities by other government/camps, "The Nazis were only found (or remembered as) guilty because they lost the war."

### 2 - Introduction to Concentration Camp Resistance

#### Absolute Subjugation

>The camps were so ordered against resistance that merely to lift one's hand in defense of an incoming blow was considered a grievous act of defiance, worthy of torturous execution.

The "concentration universe" was carefully designed to suppress all resistance, shaming people who didn't act is therefore inappropriate.

>These are not stories of individual passivity - they are stories of systematic disempowerment.

#### Precluding Resistance

Starvation was one of the main tools for crushing resistance.
Another was social alienation, the triangles and stars on the inmates uniforms played a role in this :

>Because these identity categories came to be so internalized and cherished by the inmates, connections between inmates were inherently governed by Nazi strategy. The d i fferential treatment of these artificial groupings created deep fissures between prisoners.

Inmates were competing for positions of privilege (Capo, non-lethal labor, extra food etc).
Demoralization and propaganda (war is over, no one is coming to save you) was also used.
Daily life filled with cruel and bizarre laws : "This was a universe in which the SS would often pro­vide costly health care to one of their torture victims only to send them to the gas chambers upon recovery".

#### The Conditions for Resistance

Even survivors of all these conditions who have the will and capacity to resist were faced with other mechanisms.
Example : the policy of collective responsibility : "any act of revolt, sabotage, or escape was met with brutal punishment, not only for
those involved, but for an arbitrary selection of other inmates ." This led some resistance groups to adopt a "no-escape" policy.
Another example : the SS network of snitches, easy to obtain through competition. The SS even put a "snitch-box" where inmates could anonymously rat each other out.
And still :

>In spite of these and a host of other factors that destroyed almost any possibility of resistance, we are nevertheless faced with a rich history of sabotage, insurrection, mutual aid, escape, spontaneous defiance, and underground organization within the camps.

>In one case, castor oil was put into an informant's soup, and when admitted to the hospital he was given a lethal in­jection by a doctor who was part of the resistance.

(other good examples pages 33 to 34)
Serafinsky cherrish that resistance happens instead of dwelling on the question of passivity, and draws parrallels with current struggles.
"Everything could be treated as resistance because everything was prohibited." Mutal aid, medical aid, communication with the outside, charity, friendship, cultural contributions, even poetry and songs.

>In keeping with the an­ anarcho-nihilist tendency, this essay is a bout those who attacked. In each of the following sections, one form of concentration camp resistance will be paired with an exploration of anarcho-nihilist ideas

### 3 - Sabotage and Pure Negation

After the spring of 1942, slave labor was used in camps for nearby factories. Sabotage occurred, not for increasing chances of inmates survival or escape, but for the negation of Nazism. "It is not the outcome of the act, but the moment of action itself that speaks loudest here."
Negation and jouissance, two nihilist concepts, resonate within these acts of sabotage.
Anti sabotage took the forms of violence, benefits for highly productive inmates, and finally "shared responsibility". Sabotage occurred anyway.

#### Sabotage in the Lagers

The author detail examples of weapon sabotage, often masked as accidents. Sabotage was sometimes spontaneous, sometimes organized.
Inmates assigned with bureaucratic work could send skilled worker to do unskilled work and vice-versa.

#### Pure Negation

Reminder of the contrast between AnNihil and anarchism with "positive programs" for the future. Nihilist see visions about the future unproductive and even dangerous : "Such forward-looking conversations attempt to herd an infinite spectrum of possibilities  onto an ideal anarchist path." The goal of negation is more urgent.
Nihilism claims that there is a common trap : positive identification within society. Example : "when we are not rioting, we are community organizers, Food Not Bombs, etc...". Negation (and so the riots) however, is justified because the ruling order exist.

#### Jouissance

Long definition of Jouissance, last phrase : "Is is the visceral experience of negation as ecstatic liberation".

>Through this lens, the joy of pissing in a Nazi rocket cannot easily be measured against its risks or results

Jouissance puts emphasis on the act rather than on its outcomes.

### 4 - Spontaneous Resistance and Time

The concept of time is recurrent in both AnNihil and Holocaust literature.

#### Spontaneous Resistance in the Lagers

Details of several spontaneous acts of revolt.

#### Lager-time. Despondency. and Timelessness

Again, emphasis put on the act rather than the result. "the very fact that each story ends with a mass slaughter gestures towards a spirit of resistance that prioritized lived revolt over futurity."
How we understand time is key to resistance both in the Lagers and in today's existing order.
Survival in camps meant total immersion in the present, forgetting both past and future. Some survivors even forgot life before the camp.

#### Anarcho-Nihilism . Progressivism . and Futurity

AnNihil reject the idea of progressivism (things are getting better). Some thinkers blame Marxism for it.
As Baeden reminds us : "any progressive development can only mean a more so­sophisticated system of misery and exploitation".
Capitalism has now recuperated this socialist mode of thinking, and the image of The Child is the ultimate symbol of our commitment to the future :

>Through this lens, the widely-felt social pressure to have children is actually an obligation to reproduce society and capitalism.

#### Messianic Time

A concept from Walter Benjamin. Messianic time is an "irrational now-time", an "interruption of linear time". It negates both the lies of progressivism/futurity and the sense of absolute despair.
End of chapter :

>Messianic time is the chronological awareness in which jouissance can flourish , for rather than deferring our rage to the future we can finally realize that now is the time we've been waiting for.

### 5 - Organizations and Major Uprisings

#### Overview of Organizations in Lagers

AnNihil is known for its critique of organizations, the author will offer stories of three mass uprisings in camps with that critique in mind.

#### The Resistance Movement in Auschwitz & The Sonder kommando Uprising

Witold Pilecki, a Polish soldier volunteered to be sent in camps to organize resistance.
In Auschwitz, Sonderkommando workers approach The Fighting Group (one of the resistance organizations) to craft a plan for revolt. The response was that "the time is not ripe for a general uprising". They didn't have time for this stategical tact.
"In the end, the resistance organizations spent years organizing for a general uprising that never happened.". Instead, an informal desesperate group staged the only coordinated insurection in that camp history.

#### Anarcho-Nihilist Critique of Organizations

AnNihil can be seen as an anti-social turn in anarchist thought that doesn't wait for a mass, consensual movment that will probably never happens, and "pri­oritizes immediate attack rooted in individual desires."
Comparaisons between the collective<>individual tension in anarchist circles and the same tension in Auschwitz.
Because of repression on other anarchists/inmates, there is still a notion of collective responsibility that nihilism wrestles with, even without organizations.

#### The Sobibor Uprising

Description of the uprising.

>Between forty and sixty participants in the uprising survived the war, which along with four others who had escaped earlier, are the only known survivors of Sobibor.

#### The Treblinka Uprising

Description of the uprising.

>As in Sobibor, successful escape meant the beginning of a new, even more dangerous mission: to survive the hostile countryside. In the end, 52 of the rebels lived to see the end of the war and tell the world about Treblinka.

#### Anarcho-Nihilism and Informal Organizing

About the "ripe conditions" :

>Once again, a great deal of patience was required to strike at the right moment. The difference between deferral and patience rests on one's proven commitment to action.

The uprisings resonate with AnNihil organizations, which are leaderless, informal, autonomous etc... The author use the examples of CCF/FAI and the ITS to illustrate his point.
The rejection of inherited programs, moralities, expectations etc gives AnNihils the tactical freedom necessary for this mode of organization. Hopefully, this lead to organizing being a joyful thing instead of a bureaucratic affair.
The fact remains that the two most succesful uprisings happened in the only camps with no formal organizations.
Serafinski add nuance and find some pros and cons to both modes of organizing. The problem of "collective responsibility" still remains, and so he writes :

>Ultimately somebody along the line is going to have to make shady ethical choices, regardless of organizational model.

### 6 - Reflections

#### Cruel Optimisms

A definition from the book "Cruel Optimism" by Lauren Berlant :

>a relation of attachment to compro­mised conditions of possibility whose realization is discovered either to be impossible, sheer fantasy, or too possible, and toxic.

In the camps, those cruel optimisms took the forms of rumors about the war ending soon or partisan help on the way. It was "both a lifeline for desperate people, and a perpetual deterrent for resistance."

Again a parralel to our current situation, like the attachement to the old idea of "building the new world in the shell of the old".

#### Insurrectionary Memories

Emphasis on remembering these struggles to fuel the current ones.

#### The Void

>Though we have inherited a great many ideas about how to confront domination, we know that nothing is set in stone. From the shattered tools and bones of our predecessors, we craft our own weapons. Nothing is guaranteed to work, yet we attack regard­less. We do so naked, having shed the rags of morality, ideology, and politics that had accumulated over time. We confront this world raw, in all its horrifying glory. We negate every truth and rule and we proceed with a spirit of incendiary experimentation. We dream big, ex­pect little, and celebrate every moment of rupture. We take every opportunity to ensure that those in power lose sleep and that their functionaries have miserable jobs. We set our lives to ripping up the geraniums that line the extermination camp paths, pissing in the gears of society's machinery, and when all else fails, we will follow in the footsteps of those who spent their final minutes in the gas chambers singing and fucking.
May jouissance be the blessed flame that guides us into the void.
